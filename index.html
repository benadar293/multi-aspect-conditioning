Dual Performance-Transcription Conditioning for Diffusion-Based Multi-Instrument Music Synthesis

Ben Maman1, Johannes Zeitler2, Meinard Müller2, Amit H. Bermano1,
1Tel Aviv University
2International Audio Laboratories Erlangen, Germany
Supplementary Data Distribution Repo arXiv

Abstract

Generating multi-instrument music from symbolic music representations is an important task in Music Information Retrieval (MIR). A central challenge in this context is musical and acoustic control in the generation process. That is, control over notes, timbre, and further acoustic properties. Previous works are limited to datasets synthesized by concatenative synthesis, due to lack of real datasets, and lack control over acoustics, other than simple instrument selection. As the main contribution of this work, we propose enhancing both realism and acoustic control, by training a diffusion model on real multi-instrument musical performances of diverse performers and instrumentation, while conditioning the model on acoustics of a specific performance. To this end, we introduce performance conditioning -- a tool indicating the generative model to synthesize music with the acoustics, style and timbre of specific instruments, taken from specific performances. The acoustic characteristics are implicitly defined by a set of recordings, e.g., tracks of a single CD, sharing the same acoustic conditions. We show this tool is effective in a wide range of scenarios -- generating subtle differences in timbre of an instrument (e.g., different types of church organs), generating different room acoustics, style transfer, or even implicitly generating instrumentation from simple pitch conditions. For musical control, we show that state-of-the art transcription and alignment techniques are effective in providing note and instrument conditions for the diffusion model. Most significantly, this enables training on diverse, uncurated, real-world multi-instrument musical performances, including orchestral symphonies, which is yet to be done in previous work, to the best of our knowledge. We evaluate our prototype on uncurated performances of diverse instrumentation, with extensive listening tests and established score metrics (FAD), evaluating the different aspects of synthesis - realism, perceptual similarity to the target performance, and accuracy of the musical content. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance while allowing novel timbre and style control. Our project page, including samples and demonstrations, is available at benadar293.github.io/midipm.


Contribution

This work complements and expands our ICASSP 2024 paper. There are 3 significant improvements in this work:
  • For note conditions we use predictions of a transcriber, rather than alignments. This is practicaly more favourable, since unaligned MIDIs do not always exist.
  • We show that the model can generate performances from pitch-only note conditions, while performance conditioning implicitly controls instrumentation.
  • We perform extensive listening tests, evaluating both realism, and similarity to the target performance.

Pop, Rock, and Jazz Music

In this section we provide samples of performances generated by a synthesizer trained on rock and pop music albums. For note conditions, we used predicted transcriptions of an existing transcriber. In the model used in this section, all training performances are unknown to the transcriber. The transcriber was trained mainly on rock albums, with unalinged MIDIs, following Maman and Bermano, 2022. However, instrumentation in MIDIs of pop and rock music is extremely prone to inaccuracies and incosistencies, and we therefore rely on the synthesizer's generative power, and on performance conditioning, to produce likely instrumentation. Improving instrument disentanglement in such genres is an important direction for future work, and we believe improved transcription will enable more control in the generation process.

Note the ability of the model to generate drums, as the MIDI input contains only pitch and instrument (without drums).
The model can generate human voice, but it is not conditioned on lyrics - this is important future work.

Comparison

Our main comparison is with Hawthorne et al. (2022). Although we use the same T5 backbone, there are 2 main differences between the models:
(1) Data - We train on real data alone (~58 hours), including orchestral symphonies, while Hawthorne et al. train on mainly synthetic data (~1500 hours).
(2) We incorporate performance conditioning.

For qualitative comparison with Hawthorne et al. (2022), see our ICASSP 2024 paper's project page.
